Water intoxication or hyponatremia is a dangerous condition from drinking too much water too fast, **diluting blood sodium levels**, causing cells (especially brain cells) to swell (cerebral edema), leading to symptoms like headache, nausea, confusion, seizures, coma, and potentially death, often seen in athletes or infants, preventable by drinking to thirst and avoiding excessive intake.

This occurs when sodium levels drop below 135 mmol/L, which can happen when athletes consume large amounts of fluid.

At the onset of this condition, fluid outside the cells has an excessively low amount of solutes, such as sodium and other electrolytes, in comparison to the fluid inside the cells, causing the fluid to move into the cells to balance its [[Osmotic Concentration]]. This causes the cells to swell. The swelling increases intracranial pressure in the brain, which leads to the first observable symptoms of water intoxication: headache, personality changes, changes in behavior, confusion, irritability, and drowsiness.
