**Alkaloids** are a large group of naturally occurring organic compounds that mostly contain **nitrogen atoms** and are **produced by plants, fungi, bacteria, and some animals**.

Alkaloids often have **shapes and chemical properties** similar to our body’s **neurotransmitters, hormones, or signaling molecules** (like dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, and adrenaline).

Because of that, they can **bind to the same receptors** in the nervous system or other tissues and either:

- **Activate them** → stimulating an effect
- **Block them** → preventing the normal signal

For example:
- **Morphine** mimics endorphins (the body’s natural painkillers) → binds to opioid receptors → blocks pain signals → causes euphoria and pain relief.
- **Atropine** blocks acetylcholine receptors → reduces muscle spasms and slows certain nerve signals.

Many alkaloids affect the central nervous system (CNS) because they can cross the blood–brain barrier.
They may:
- Stimulate (like caffeine, nicotine, cocaine)
- Depress or calm (like morphine, scopolamine)
- Distort perception (like mescaline, psilocybin, though these are hallucinogenic alkaloids)

Their nitrogen makes them reactive
- Form strong chemical bonds with biological molecules (like proteins and DNA)
- Give them basic (alkaline) properties → helps them interact with acidic body molecules
- This reactivity contributes to their strong biological activity, even in tiny doses.
