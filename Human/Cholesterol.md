Cholesterol is a lipid (fat-like molecule) found in every cell of your body.
It has a waxy, ring-shaped structure, and it's used as a building block to make many important substances, including:

- **Cell membranes** → it keeps them flexible and stable
- **Vitamin D**, Cholesterol is converted to Vitamin D3. Needs sunlight + liver & kidney activation.
- **Bile acids** → help digest fats
- **Steroid hormones** → such as cortisol, testosterone, estrogen, and DHEA

So, without cholesterol, your body couldn’t make any of the steroid hormones we just talked about.

Because cholesterol doesn't dissolve in water (or blood), it travels through the bloodstream packaged with proteins, forming what we call lipoproteins:

| Type                               | What it does                                                        | Common nickname  |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| **LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)**  | Carries cholesterol *to* cells. Too much can stick to artery walls. | Bad cholesterol  |
| **HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein)** | Carries excess cholesterol *away* from tissues back to the liver.   | Good cholesterol |

> [!IMPORTANT] 
> It’s not that cholesterol itself is bad, it’s the **balance** between LDL and HDL that matters for cardiovascular health.

## Where it comes from

- Liver makes most of your cholesterol naturally.
- The rest comes from food, especially animal products (meat, eggs, dairy).
- The body tightly regulates how much it makes, if you eat more, it usually produces less.
